Demand for data storage is growing exponentially, but the capacity of existing storage media is not keeping up. However, DNA-based storage is extremely dense, above 1EB/mm3, and long-lasting, over 500 years. Therefore, in this research project, I want to do a simulation experiment that recovered a 600 kB message without error from a DNA-based storage system.

DNA consists of four types of nucleotides: adenine, cytosine, guanine, and thymine. The storage system includes a DNA synthesizer, PCR Thermocycler, and DNA Sequencer. DNA synthesizer encodes the data to be stored in DNA by using XOR encoding. PCR thermocycler amplifies the concentration of selected sequences of DNA within a pool. DNA Sequencer reads DNA sequences and converts them back into digital data. The basic unit of DNA storage is a 50 to 100-bit DNA strand in pools.

To complete the project successfully, it is necessary to understand Polymerase chain reaction(PCR) and Key-value architecture. DNA sequencing primers can define the beginning and end of the region to be amplified. PCR can amplify desired data based on DNA sequencing primers, which gives us enough pieces of the same DNA data to study. (Link to PCR video). Without PCR, we need to go through and decode all DNA strands in the data pool to find the data we want. Too costly.
PCR explanation video

Furthermore, Key-value Architecture can let us map a key to a pair of primers. In short, at write time, those primers are added to DNA strands. At read time, those same primers are used in PCR to amplify desired keys.

In a nutshell, the area of DNA data storage is relatively new and has not reached the level of widespread application since the cost of synthesis and sequencing due to the current biotechnology. However, the potential in this field is great, helping the entire human civilization store a colossal amount of necessary data. DNA-data storage paper
